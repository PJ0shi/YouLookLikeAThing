What is AI?

:to refer to a particular style of computer program called a machine learning algorithm. Since we don’t write their rules, they come up with their own, and they don’t write them down or explain them the way a human would. Instead, the AIs make complex interdependent adjustments to their own internal structures, turning a generic framework into something that’s fine-tuned for an individual task.

Artificial neural networks

What: Artificial neural networks are imitation brains. They’re all loosely modeled after the way the brain works. They’re built from a bunch of simple chunks of software, each able to perform very simple math. These chunks are usually called cells or neurons, an analogy with the neurons that make up our own brains. The power of the neural network lies in how these cells are connected.

How: Initially, the AI gives random wieghts to the ceels in the network, but learns to assign the correct weights based on iterative feedback from 'judges'. In a neural network that trains itself, using trial and error to adjust the connections between cells, it’s usually a lot harder to identify each cell’s job. Tasks tend to be spread among several cells—and in the case of some cells, it’s difficult or impossible to tell what tasks they accomplish.

When:image recognition, some for language processing, some for generating music, some for optimizing the productivity of a cockroach farm, some for writing confusing jokes among many other things

Random Forests

What: A random forest algorithm is made of individual units called decision trees. A decision tree is basically a flowchart that leads to an outcome based on the information we have. And, pleasingly, decision trees do kind of look like upside-down trees.

How: A random forest is made of a bunch of tiny (that is, shallow) trees that each consider a tiny bit of information to make a couple of small decisions. During the training process, each shallow tree learns which information to pay attention to and what the outcome should be. Each tiny tree’s decision probably won’t be very good, because it’s based on very limited information. But if all the tiny trees in the forest pool their decisions and vote on the final outcome, they will be much more accurate than any individual tree.

When: prediction and classification—predicting customer behavior, for example, or making book recommendations or judging the quality of a wine—based on a bunch of input data.

Evolutionary Algorithms

What:Evolutionary algorithms mimic the process of natural evolution to solve complex problems.

How:The algorithm begins by defining the aspects of the solution that can vary and those that remain constant. A fitness function is established to evaluate the performance of each solution.Simulations are then used to test the solutions. The algorithm proceeds by selecting the best solutions and allowing them to reproduce. This can happen through processes like mutation, where random changes are introduced to the solutions, or crossover, where traits from multiple solutions are combined to create new ones. Through successive generations, the algorithm refines the solutions, discarding less successful ones and favoring those that exhibit improved performance. Over time, innovative strategies and solutions emerge that better address the problem being tackled.

When: Car bumpers that dissipate force when they crumple, proteins that bind to other medically useful proteins, flywheels that spin just so—these are all problems that people have used evolutionary algorithms to solve.

General Adversarial Networks

What:The key thing about GANs is they’re really two algorithms in one—two adversaries that learn by testing each other.

How: One, the generator, tries to imitate the input dataset. The other, the discriminator, tries to tell the difference between the generator’s imitation and the real thing. The GAN will learn more quickly if we give it very limited, consistent datasets and show the same subject in the same pose using a plain, consistent background. Otherwise the GAN will spend a long time trying to learn when and how to draw other background elements that may appear in the training images.

When: Image-generating, image-remixing, and image-filtering - turning a summer scene into a winter one, generating faces of imaginary people, or changing a photo of someone’s cat into a cubist painting
